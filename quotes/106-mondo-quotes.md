**Part 1, Gross Out Intro**

- [ ] Sid Krichheimer, [South Florida Sentinel](https://www.newspapers.com/image/236577840/), “Today’s cringing parents were yesterday's pranksters, getting their jollies from at their parents' expense with disgusting fake vomit, vampire blood, and whoopee cushions.”  
- [ ] Google Gemini, “The gross-out toy craze of the 1980s was primarily driven by the deregulation of children's television advertising, changing cultural norms, and a lucrative market for shock value. These intentionally revolting playthings allowed kids to rebel against the polished, wholesome toys of previous generations. \[[1](https://www.instagram.com/reel/DWBp5-EDU8A/), [2](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/toys-and-games-1980s), [3](https://www.reddit.com/r/generationology/comments/swlqm6/why_were_most_animated_shows_in_the_80s_only_made/)\]”  
- [ ] Google Gemini, Reasons included, Deregulation and Media Tie-ins: In the early 1980s, the U.S. government relaxed Federal Communications Commission (FCC) regulations, allowing toy companies to create animated series specifically designed to sell merchandise. With toy aisles flooding with standard superheroes and robots, unique "gross-out" products—like Garbage Pail Kids or Madballs—stood out.  
- [ ] Google Gemini, Shock Value and Rebellion: Toys dripping with slime, peeling skin, and deformed faces were a fun, harmless way for children to rebel against adult sensibilities. Because parents often found these toys repulsive, they became a badge of honor and a symbol of independence for kids.  
- [ ] Google Gemini, Profitable Saturation: Brands like Wacky Packages had already proven that kids loved crude humor. When lines like Garbage Pail Kids (a parody of the wholesome Cabbage Patch Kids) became wildly successful, toy and card manufacturers realized that "nasty was profitable".  
- [ ] Google Gemini, Creativity in Manufacturing: New plastics and foam molding technologies made it cheap and easy to mass-produce squishy, textured, and odd-looking novelty toys.
